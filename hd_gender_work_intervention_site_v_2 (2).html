DIGITAL INTERVENTION PROJECT

WHO GETS
LEFT OUT?

Workplace equality often fails the workers most affected by precarious labour. This project explores invisibility, inequality, and exclusion within gendered labour systems.

Explore the Project
THE INVISIBLE WORKFORCE
Casual workers.
Part-time workers.
Carers.
Invisible labour.

Women are disproportionately affected by precarious forms of employment including casual labour, insecure contracts, part-time work, and unpaid caring responsibilities.

These labour structures contribute to lower wages, reduced job security, limited career progression, and long-term financial instability.

01

Lower Income

Precarious labour often results in unstable income and reduced long-term financial security.

02

Job Insecurity

Temporary and casual employment structures create uncertainty and vulnerability.

03

Care Burden

Women continue to carry disproportionate unpaid caring responsibilities.

04

Structural Inequality

Workplace equality policies remain unevenly implemented across industries.

THE STRUCTURAL GAP

Equality Is Not Equally Accessible.

Our research identified a structural mismatch between existing workplace equity programs and the workers most affected by precarious employment.

Grace Papers primarily operates through employer partnerships, leadership coaching, and workplace advisory systems. However, workers outside formal employment structures are often the least likely to access these forms of support.

OUR INTERVENTION

Beyond Corporate Equality.

Expand Accessibility

Increase access through more community-based and in-person support systems.

Broaden Partnerships

Collaborate with NGOs, unions, and community organisations.

Public Advocacy

Advocate for stronger labour protections and gender-responsive reforms.

Structural Change

Address wider cultural and social systems shaping labour inequality.

“Gender inequality in precarious work cannot be solved through individual resilience alone.”
CALL TO ACTION

Equality Must Reach Everyone.

Addressing gendered precarious work requires collaboration between employers, policymakers, organisations, and communities.

Developed as part of a Gender at Work intervention project in collaboration with Grace Papers.
