WHO GETS
LEFT OUT?

Workplace equality policies often fail the workers most affected by precarious labour. This digital intervention explores how gendered precarious work continues to shape inequality, invisibility, and exclusion within labour systems.

Explore the Project

The Invisible Workforce

Casual workers.
Part-time workers.
Carers.
Invisible labour.

Women are disproportionately affected by precarious forms of employment including casual work, insecure contracts, part-time employment, and unpaid caring responsibilities. These labour structures contribute to lower wages, reduced job security, limited career progression, and long-term financial instability.

Lower Income

Precarious labour often results in unstable and inconsistent income, reducing long-term financial security for women workers.

Job Insecurity

Casual and temporary employment structures create uncertainty and increase vulnerability within labour systems.

Care Burden

Women continue to carry disproportionate unpaid caring responsibilities, limiting workforce participation and progression.

Structural Inequality

Workplace equality policies remain unevenly implemented across industries and organisational cultures.

Why This Matters

Despite increasing public conversations around workplace equality, many support systems continue to rely heavily on formal employment structures. Workers in precarious positions are often the least likely to access organisational support, professional development, or workplace flexibility.

Gender

Workplace inequality remains embedded within labour systems.

Care

Unpaid care work continues to shape workforce inequality.

The Structural Gap

Our research identified a structural mismatch between existing workplace equity programs and the workers most affected by precarious employment.

Employer-Based Systems

Grace Papers primarily works through employer partnerships, leadership coaching, and organisational advisory support.

Limited Accessibility

Workers outside formal workplace systems are often excluded from support structures and workplace resources.

Invisible Exclusion

Those most affected by gendered precarious work may be the least visible within workplace equality initiatives.

Beyond Corporate Equality

This intervention proposes expanding workplace equality initiatives beyond corporate consultation models. Structural inequality cannot be addressed through organisational reform alone — broader social systems and community engagement must also be considered.

Expand Accessibility

Increase access to support through more community-based and in-person services.

Broaden Partnerships

Collaborate with NGOs, unions, and community organisations to reach vulnerable workers.

Public Advocacy

Advocate for governments and policymakers to adopt stronger labour protections and gender-responsive reforms.

Structural Change

Address the broader cultural and social systems shaping gendered labour inequality.

“Gender inequality in precarious work cannot be solved through individual resilience alone. Structural change requires collective responsibility.”

Equality Must Reach Everyone

Addressing gendered precarious work requires collaboration between employers, policymakers, organisations, and communities. Awareness alone is not enough — meaningful structural change must follow.

For Employers

Build inclusive workplace systems that support flexibility, progression, and accessibility for all workers.

For Policymakers

Strengthen labour protections and support gender-responsive workplace reforms.

For Communities

Support advocacy initiatives and amplify discussions surrounding gender equity and labour rights.

About This Project

This digital intervention was developed as part of a Gender at Work project in collaboration with Grace Papers. The website translates academic research into a public-facing awareness and advocacy platform exploring the structural inequalities of gendered precarious work.

References include Connell (1990), Goldin (2014), Horler & Joffe (2022), Paraskevopoulou (2020), and WGEA reports (2025–2026).
